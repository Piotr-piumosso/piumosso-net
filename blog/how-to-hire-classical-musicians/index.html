How to Hire Classical Musicians for Your Corporate Event: A Practical Guide

By Piotr Górski, Più Mosso May 2026 9 min read

Most event planners approach live classical music the way they approach catering: find a supplier, confirm availability, sign a contract. It usually works. But when it doesn't — when the quartet plays at the wrong volume during speeches, when the programme feels generic, when the musicians seem surprised by the venue — the problem almost always traces back to a brief that was too thin, or a booking made too fast.

This guide is written from the musician's side of that relationship. These are the things we wish every event director knew before picking up the phone.

1. Decide what you actually need — before you call anyone

Classical music is a broad category. Before contacting an ensemble, answer these four questions for yourself:

  • What is the primary function of the music? Background atmosphere during a reception is a different brief than a featured 30-minute concert after dinner. Both are valid; they require completely different programming and volume calibration.
  • Who are your guests? A room of 200 senior finance executives has different expectations than a product launch for a tech company or a hospital foundation gala. Age, nationality, and industry all affect what will land well.
  • What is the acoustic reality of the venue? A glass-and-steel event space with no soft furnishings is a very different environment than a historic ballroom. This determines what instruments and repertoire will work — or won't.
  • What is the timing structure? Is the music running during dinner, before speeches, during cocktail hour, or as a standalone performance? Overlapping with speeches is the most common mistake event planners make — and the most avoidable one.
The most expensive mistake: booking music for "background" without specifying volume. A pianist who fills a concert hall and a pianist who supports conversation are sometimes the same person — and sometimes not. Clarify this in writing before you book.

2. Understand acoustic requirements before choosing an ensemble

Not every instrument works in every space. This is not a matter of talent — it is physics.

Room size and instrument choice

A string quartet fills a 150-person ballroom beautifully without amplification. In a 400-person conference hall with a hard ceiling, the same ensemble will struggle to project without technical support — which changes the character of the performance entirely. A piano and violin duo can calibrate more precisely to a room's needs than a larger ensemble.

The amplification question

If your venue requires amplification, clarify this early. Many classical musicians do not use microphones by design — acoustic performance is part of the artistic offer. If you need amplification (for a very large room, or for guests who need audio support), say so when you enquire. It affects the booking, the setup time, and the programme.

What to tell the musician

Share the room dimensions, the ceiling height, the number of guests, and whether there are hard reflective surfaces (windows, stone floors, glass walls). A professional ensemble will tell you immediately whether the venue works and what adaptations are needed.

3. What to look for in an ensemble — beyond the recordings

Recordings tell you about musical quality. They do not tell you about reliability, communication, or operational flexibility — which matter more at a corporate event than at a concert hall.

When evaluating an ensemble, look for:

  • Corporate event experience specifically. Playing for a concert audience is different from playing in a room where people are eating, talking, and checking their phones. Ask for references from events, not just concerts.
  • Programme flexibility. Can they adapt repertoire to your brief, or do they play the same programme everywhere? The best ensembles build the programme around your event — not the other way around.
  • Communication speed and clarity. How quickly do they respond to your initial enquiry? How detailed is their first reply? This is a strong signal of how they will operate on the day.
  • Logistical self-sufficiency. Do they handle their own travel, setup, and instrument logistics — or will they need extensive coordination from your team on the day?

4. How to write a brief that gets you the right programme

Most event briefs contain a date, a venue name, and a duration. That is enough to confirm availability — it is not enough to produce a good programme. Send the musician this information before they quote:

  • Event type and occasion (gala, award ceremony, product launch, client dinner)
  • Guest profile: industry, seniority, approximate age range, nationalities
  • Venue name and a link, or floor plan if available
  • Timing: when music starts, when it ends, and what happens around it (speeches, dinner service, dancing)
  • Desired atmosphere: one word or phrase — "understated elegance", "celebratory", "intimate and conversational"
  • Any specific repertoire preferences or exclusions (some clients request no film music; others specifically want it)

A professional ensemble will use this to propose a programme. If they come back with a standard list regardless of your brief, that is useful information.

5. The budget conversation — what the fee actually covers

Live music at a corporate event is priced differently from DJ or AV services. Understanding what you are paying for prevents surprises.

A musician's fee typically covers:

  • Programme development — researching, selecting and arranging repertoire for your specific event. For a bespoke 90-minute programme, this can be 6–10 hours of preparation invisible to the client.
  • Rehearsal time — a professional ensemble rehearses specifically for each event, not just runs through their standard set.
  • Performance time — the actual hours at your event.
  • Travel and logistics — instrument transport, accommodation if needed, travel time.
A note on pricing: when a quote seems unusually low, ask what is included. Programme that has not been developed for your event, musicians who are unfamiliar with each other, and no pre-event venue visit all reduce the fee — and the quality.

6. Confirm these seven things before signing

  • Programme in writing — specific works, approximate durations
  • Sound requirements — amplification yes/no, who provides equipment
  • Setup and soundcheck time needed (arrive how early?)
  • Space requirements — footprint, electrical outlets, lighting needs
  • Cancellation and postponement policy
  • What happens if a musician is ill — substitution policy
  • Contact person on the day — mobile number, not just email

One final point

The best corporate music experiences are not the ones where the music was loudest, most technically impressive, or most expensive. They are the ones where guests forgot they were listening — and felt something instead. That outcome requires a musician who understands your event, not just their instrument.

The brief, the acoustic preparation, and the programme development are not paperwork. They are the product.
